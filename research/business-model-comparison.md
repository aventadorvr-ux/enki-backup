# Business Model Comparison: IP Sale vs SaaS
## NZ Real Estate AI Agent System

**Date:** March 12, 2026  
**Analysis by:** Enki AI  
**Purpose:** Compare IP sale model vs SaaS recurring revenue model

---

## Executive Summary

Two viable business models for the NZ Real Estate AI Agent System:
1. **SaaS Model** (Original): $299-999/agent/month recurring
2. **IP Sale Model** (New): Sell IP to companies for large upfront fee

**Recommendation:** Hybrid approach - SaaS for majority, IP sale for 1-2 enterprise clients as proof of concept.

---

## Model 1: SaaS (Software as a Service)

### How It Works
- We own and operate the platform
- Agents pay monthly subscription ($299-999/agent)
- We control product roadmap, updates, support
- Direct relationship with end users

### Revenue Projections (5 Years)

| Year | Agents | Avg Price/Month | Monthly Revenue | Annual Revenue |
|------|--------|-----------------|-----------------|----------------|
| 1 | 50 | $400 | $20,000 | $240,000 |
| 2 | 150 | $450 | $67,500 | $810,000 |
| 3 | 400 | $500 | $200,000 | $2,400,000 |
| 4 | 800 | $550 | $440,000 | $5,280,000 |
| 5 | 1,500 | $600 | $900,000 | $10,800,000 |

**5-Year Total:** ~$19.5M ARR at exit

### Pros

**Financial:**
- ✅ Predictable recurring revenue (MRR)
- ✅ Higher lifetime value per customer
- ✅ Compound growth potential
- ✅ Valuable for acquisition (5-10x revenue multiple)

**Strategic:**
- ✅ Full control over product
- ✅ Direct customer feedback loop
- ✅ Can pivot quickly
- ✅ Build brand equity
- ✅ Own the customer relationship

**Operational:**
- ✅ Continuous improvement
- ✅ Can serve small agencies (not just big ones)
- ✅ Lower barrier to entry for customers

### Cons

**Financial:**
- ❌ Slower initial cash flow
- ❌ High customer acquisition cost (CAC)
- ❌ Churn risk (customers can leave)
- ❌ Need ongoing sales/marketing investment

**Operational:**
- ❌ Support burden scales with users
- ❌ Infrastructure costs grow
- ❌ 24/7 uptime requirements
- ❌ Competition can undercut pricing

**Strategic:**
- ❌ Requires ongoing investment
- ❌ Market risk (slow adoption)

## Model 2: IP Sale (Intellectual Property)

### How It Works
- Sell full system IP + source code to real estate company
- Company rebrands as their own ("Harcourts AI Pro")
- Company mandates all agents MUST use their branded system
- Agents pay company $2,000-5,000 one-time (or monthly to company)
- Company owns all data, customization, future development

### Revenue Scenarios

#### Scenario A: Sell to 1 Major Company

| Buyer | Upfront Price | Royalty | Year 1 Total | 5-Year Total |
|-------|---------------|---------|--------------|--------------|
| Harcourts (2,000 agents) | $500,000 | 5% of agent fees | $500,000 + $100,000 = $600,000 | $500K + $500K royalty = $1M |

**Result:** Quick $500K-1M, then done.

#### Scenario B: Sell to 3 Companies

| Buyers | Upfront | Royalty | Total |
|--------|---------|---------|-------|
| Harcourts + Ray White + Barfoot | $300K each = $900K | 3% each | $900K + $540K/year = $1.44M Year 1 |

**5-Year Total:** ~$3.6M

#### Scenario C: Sell to All 7 Majors

| Buyers | Upfront | Royalty | Total |
|--------|---------|---------|-------|
| All 7 companies | $200K each = $1.4M | 2% each | $1.4M + $1.12M/year |

**5-Year Total:** ~$7M

### Pros

**Financial:**
- ✅ Large upfront payment (immediate cash)
- ✅ No ongoing support burden
- ✅ Fast exit/cash event
- ✅ Can fund next venture

**Strategic:**
- ✅ Companies have incentive to enforce usage (captive market)
- ✅ Companies handle all agent billing/churn
- ✅ No competition with buyers
- ✅ Simpler business model

**Operational:**
- ✅ No infrastructure to maintain
- ✅ No support tickets
- ✅ No uptime requirements

### Cons

**Financial:**
- ❌ One-time payment vs recurring
- ❌ Lower total value over 5 years
- ❌ No ongoing revenue stream
- ❌ Limited to companies that can afford IP purchase

**Strategic:**
- ❌ Loss of control over product direction
- ❌ Can't improve the product
- ❌ No customer feedback
- ❌ Risk of company not executing well
- ❌ Brand doesn't grow

**Operational:**
- ❌ Complex negotiations
- ❌ Legal/compliance transfer costs
- ❌ May need non-compete agreements

## Critical Risk Analysis

### SaaS Risks

**High Impact:**
- Slow adoption (NZ market conservative)
- High churn if product doesn't deliver ROI
- Competition from big tech (Zillow, etc.)
- Economic downturn reduces agent spending

**Mitigation:**
- Start with free trial/pilot programs
- Focus on measurable ROI (leads generated, time saved)
- Build moat through NZ-specific features (compliance, TradeMe)
- Keep pricing flexible

### IP Sale Risks

**High Impact:**
- Company buys IP but doesn't launch well
- Company modifies product poorly
- Market confusion (7 different "AI Pro" systems)
- Agents resist forced tool adoption
- Legal challenges (Commerce Act, anti-competitive)

**Mitigation:**
- Include performance clauses in contract
- Retain 10-20% equity/royalty stake
- Provide launch support (consulting fee)
- Limit to 2-3 exclusive licenses (not all 7)

## Market Reality Check

### Can Companies Actually Mandate Tool Usage?

**Legally:**
- In NZ, real estate agents are often independent contractors
- Companies can set technology requirements for brand consistency
- BUT agents may resist if tool is poor or expensive
- Commerce Act concerns if anti-competitive

**Practically:**
- Harcourts, Ray White already mandate CRM systems
- Agents comply because company provides leads/support
- Resistance lower if company subsidizes cost
- Success depends on tool quality + company enforcement

### Historical Precedents

**PropTech IP Sales:**
- Compass (US) acquired tech companies for $10-50M
- Zillow acquired ShowingTime for $400M (not IP, but platform)
- Most successful models are SaaS, not IP sale

**Real Estate Software:**
- MRI Software: Started as SaaS, now enterprise licensing
- CoreLogic: Mixed model (data + software subscriptions)
- Trend: Recurring revenue valued higher than one-time sales

## Hybrid Model Recommendation ⭐

### Best of Both Worlds

**Strategy:**
1. **Build SaaS platform** (keep ownership, control)
2. **Offer exclusive enterprise license** to 1 major company
   - Upfront fee: $250-500K
   - White-label rights for their brand only
   - Revenue share: 10-15% of their agent fees
   - Non-compete: Can't sell to direct competitor for 2 years
3. **Use enterprise deal as case study** to sell SaaS to others
4. **Recurring revenue** from all other agencies

### Why This Works

**Immediate Cash:**
- $250-500K upfront from enterprise deal
- Funds development, marketing, team
- Reduces need for external investment

**Validation:**
- Major company adopting = credibility
- Case study for SaaS sales
- Proof of concept for investors

**Long-Term Value:**
- Keep majority of market as SaaS
- Build brand and relationships
- Higher exit valuation

**Risk Mitigation:**
- Not dependent on single buyer
- Can pivot if enterprise deal fails
- Multiple revenue streams

## Final Recommendation

### ✅ RECOMMENDED: SaaS-First with Selective IP Licensing

**Phase 1 (Months 1-6): Build SaaS MVP**
- Develop core platform
- Get 10-20 pilot agents
- Prove product-market fit

**Phase 2 (Months 6-12): Enterprise Negotiation**
- Approach 1 major company (recommend Barfoot or Bayleys)
- Offer exclusive white-label license
- Target: $250-500K upfront + 10% royalty
- Use as case study for broader sales

**Phase 3 (Year 2+): Scale SaaS**
- Sell SaaS to all other agencies
- Use enterprise credibility
- Build to $5M+ ARR
- Position for $50M+ exit

### Why Not Pure IP Sale?

- Total 5-year value: $3-7M vs $19-26M (SaaS/hybrid)
- No ongoing business or brand
- Dependent on buyer's execution
- Can't improve or pivot

### Why Not Pure SaaS?

- Slower initial cash flow
- Higher risk if market slow to adopt
- Need capital to survive early phase
